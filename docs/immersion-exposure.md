# The exposure learner — phrases that work at the table, and what the app should do about it

Research notes for a specific (real) player: they live *inside* the language —
partner/mates and their family are native Albanian speakers, the learner is the
only non-speaker in the room, and the goal is fluency, not tourism. Their
classroom is the dinner table. This doc answers two questions:

1. **Which phrases are worth teaching first** — the ones that are actually said
   in an Albanian household *and* land with every generation, from uni-age
   cousins to grandparents?
2. **How should the app change** to serve someone whose scarce resource is not
   exposure (they have unlimited exposure) but *comprehension and permission to
   participate*?

Sources are linked inline; a gap audit against the current `DICT` is at the end.

---

## 1. The core insight: this player's needs are inverted

A tourist needs production of transactional phrases (tickets, directions,
menus) and gets almost no listening practice. This player is the opposite:

- **Listening is the bottleneck.** They hear hours of fast, connected,
  possibly dialectal Albanian daily and understand little of it. Every point of
  comprehension converts free ambient noise into free study time
  ([comprehensible-input research](https://www.carnegielearning.com/blog/comprehensible-input-and-90);
  [home-immersion study](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2215039025000104) —
  home environments work when exposure is consistent, relaxed, and daily, with
  family as *guides*, not teachers).
- **Their production targets are ritual, not transactional.** Albanian social
  life runs on fixed formulas (hospitality, toasts, blessings, thanks) that
  recur *daily with identical wording* — the family delivers perfect spaced
  repetition for free. Ritual phrases are also dialect- and generation-proof.
- **The social dynamic is the curriculum.** Announcing *po mësoj shqip* ("I'm
  learning Albanian") and asking *si thuhet…?* ("how do you say…?") converts a
  group that excludes you by accident into a room full of teachers. Community
  experience of "my partner's family speaks X" learners converges on: simple
  fixed agreements (Albanian-only at dinner) beat willpower, scripted routines
  beat open conversation, and the meta-phrases that keep the family from
  switching to English are the highest-value items in the language
  ([fluentlanguage.co.uk](https://www.fluentlanguage.co.uk/blog/tips-for-learning-language-from-girlfriend),
  [storylearning.com](https://storylearning.com/blog/dating-someone-who-speaks-a-different-language),
  [dinner-table case study](http://www.bilingualism-matters.ppls.ed.ac.uk/learning-learning-spouses-language-children-greek-family-dinner-table/)).

## 2. The phrase tiers (ranked by household utility × generational reach)

### Tier 1 — the learner's meta-kit (teach first; it buys everything else)

| Albanian | English | Why it's tier 1 |
|---|---|---|
| Po mësoj shqip | I'm learning Albanian | Converts the family into allies |
| Si thuhet … në shqip? | How do you say … in Albanian? | Infinite vocabulary faucet |
| Çfarë do të thotë …? | What does … mean? | The listening half of the faucet |
| Fol më ngadalë, të lutem | Speak slower, please | Requests slower *Albanian*, never English |
| Nuk kuptoj / E kuptova! | I don't understand / Got it! | Keeps the exchange in Albanian |
| Përsërite, të lutem / Edhe një herë | Repeat, please / one more time | — |
| Çfarë po thoni? | What are you all saying? | Group-conversation re-entry |
| A e thashë mirë? | Did I say it right? | Invites correction on the learner's terms |

The app already teaches most of this tier (*fol ngadalë*, *përsërit*, *nuk
kuptoj*, *të lutem* are all in `DICT` under the "controlling & learning
language" survival category) — the gap is the *chunks* (*si thuhet…*, *po mësoj
shqip*) and surfacing them as a kit rather than scattered words.

### Tier 2 — hospitality & table formulas (highest cultural ROI, invariant across dialects and generations)

| Albanian | English | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Urdhëro! / Urdhëroni!** | go ahead / help yourself / here you are | The single most multipurpose hospitality word: offering food, handing objects, answering the door or phone. Missing from `DICT` — the biggest single gap |
| **Të bëftë mirë!** | bon appétit ("may it do you good") | Said to anyone eating; answer: *faleminderit* |
| Hajde ha! / ha edhe pak | come on, eat! / eat a bit more | The host-insistence script — the learner hears this daily |
| Jam ngopur | I'm full | The polite refusal, *expected to be ignored once* — decline–insist–decline is a ritual, not pressure ([etiquette guide](https://albaniavisit.com/travel-guide/albanian-social-etiquette-guide/)) |
| Shumë e shijshme! | very tasty! | The cook's compliment; huge social payoff |
| Gëzuar! / Shëndet! | cheers! / to your health | Already in `DICT` (`gezuar`, `shendet`) ✓ |
| Mirë se erdhe! → **Mirë se ju gjeta** | welcome! → "well I found you" | The response is obligatory and almost no foreigner knows it — instant delight |
| A pini një kafe? | will you have a coffee? | Coffee is the atomic unit of Albanian social life; the story's kafe/raki scenes already model this ✓ |
| Faleminderit për gostinë | thanks for the hospitality | The leaving phrase |

### Tier 3 — the conversational glue (what "sounding present" is made of)

Already in `DICT`: *po, jo, mirë, hajde, dakord, epo, pra, çka, sigurisht,
ndoshta, shumë, pak, faleminderit, të lutem*. Missing but high-frequency in
real speech: **s'ka gjë / s'ka përse** (no problem / you're welcome — the
standard reply to thanks), **më fal** (sorry — `fal` exists only as "forgive"
in the blood-feud sense), **domethënë** (I mean / that is), **patjetër**
(definitely), **tamam** (exactly — Turkish loan, colloquial but used by every
generation), **bravo / të lumtë** (well done — *të lumtë* appears only inside a
folklore note).

### Tier 4 — the family itself (kinship precision + affection)

Albanian kinship terms are precise and *noticed*: **daja** (maternal uncle) vs
**xhaxha** (paternal uncle), **tezja** vs **halla** (maternal/paternal aunt),
**gjyshi/gjyshja** (grandpa/grandma). Getting these right reads as respect;
`DICT` has *nënë, baba, vëlla, motër* but none of the extended family.
Affection terms are "dialect-proof — emotional vocabulary transcends dialect
boundaries" ([albanianregistry.org](https://albanianregistry.org/blog/i-love-you-in-albanian)):
*zemër* and *shpirt* are already in `DICT` as "heart"/"soul" but not as the
vocatives they mostly are in speech (*o zemër!*); **xhan** (*të kam xhan* —
"you're dear to me", the platonic version mates actually say) and **të dua**
as a chunk are missing.

### Greetings, weighted by generation

- **Tungjatjeta** ("may you live long") — the maximally respectful hello;
  carries special weight with older Albanians. Missing from `DICT`.
- *Si je?* (informal) / *Si jeni?* (formal) — the ti/ju split in one phrase.
  Both words exist in `DICT`; the *chunk* and the register contrast are untaught.
- Everything else is already covered ✓ (*përshëndetje, mirëmëngjes, mirëdita,
  mirëmbrëma, natën e mirë, mirupafshim*, and even Gheg *tung*).

## 3. Gheg vs Tosk — "old people would get it"

**Teach standard (Tosk-based) Albanian for production; train the ear on Gheg
for reception.** The standard is what every generation was schooled in, in both
Albania and Kosovo — grandparents understand it fine
([dialect overview](https://albanianregistry.org/blog/gheg-vs-tosk-albanian-dialects)).
The trap runs the other way: most diaspora households speak **Gheg** at home,
and Gheg speech "reduces words in the standard language to barely recognisable
monosyllables" ([albaro](https://albarolanguages.com/2020/07/17/learning-to-speak-the-wrong-way-right-a-course-in-modern-gheg-albanian/)) —
a standard-trained learner can pass a course and still not recognise dinner
conversation. Recognition pairs worth teaching *receptively*:

| Meaning | Standard | Gheg (Kosovo / North) |
|---|---|---|
| How are you? | si je? | qysh je? · *a je lodh?* |
| what / what's up | çfarë / ç'kemi | çka / çka ka (`çka` is already in `DICT` ✓) |
| I want | dua | due / du |
| house | shtëpi | shpi |
| is | është | asht / osht |
| to do | të bëj | me ba / me bo |

The ritual formulas of Tier 2 are essentially invariant across dialects — one
more reason they're the safest first investment. (Conversely: don't teach
youth Anglicisms as core content; only the young "get" them.)

**Ti/ju rule of thumb** ([albanianregistry.org](https://albanianregistry.org/blog/albanian-phrases)):
*ti* with partner, peers, kids, usually your own (adoptive) grandparents; *ju*
with elderly relatives you don't know well and their friends. The contrast
shows up in pairs the app could teach as such: *si je?/si jeni?*,
*të lutem/ju lutem*, *urdhëro/urdhëroni*.

**Frequency data note:** the app's OpenSubtitles list is the right kind of
corpus — [SUBTLEX-AL](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/306528705_SUBTLEX-_AL_Albanian_word_frequencies_based_on_film_subtitles)
showed subtitle frequency models everyday spoken Albanian better than written
corpora. The true top-100 is grammar glue (*të, dhe, në, një, që, nuk…*) — all
already covered per `freqcoverage.mjs` — so the remaining wins are *chunks*,
not words.

## 4. Gap audit vs `DICT` (as of this doc)

> **Status:** the first slice is now in-game — the supper scene at the old
> woman's house (`sofra1–3`) teaches `urdhëro`, `të bëftë mirë`, `s'ka gjë`,
> the `ha edhe pak / jam ngopur` insistence ritual, `shumë e shijshme`,
> `të lumtë`, `gjyshe`, `të dua` and `të kam xhan`, and the old man in the
> square now returns a `tungjatjeta`.

Coverage is genuinely strong: top-200 frequency = 0 real gaps, survival core =
151/151. What's missing is precisely the household register:

**Missing words/chunks, in rough priority order:**
`urdhëro(ni)` · `të bëftë mirë` · `si je? / si jeni?` (as chunks + register
pair) · `mirë se erdhe / mirë se ju gjeta` · `s'ka gjë` · `më fal` (apology
sense) · `si thuhet … në shqip?` · `po mësoj shqip` · `tungjatjeta` ·
`jam ngopur` · `e shijshme` · `gjysh/gjyshe` · `daja/xhaxha/tezja/halla` ·
`xhan` · `të dua` · `të lumtë` (as praise) · `domethënë` · `patjetër` ·
`tamam` · `bravo` · `ç'kemi` · receptive Gheg pairs (`qysh`, `shpi`, `asht`,
`me ba`)

All of these have natural folk-world frames — a guest at the oda, the wedding
train (`krushqit` already walks the map), the shepherd's table, the old man's
blessing — so none require breaking the no-modern-words rule that
`survivalcore.mjs` encodes.

## 5. Making the app better for the exposure learner

Ranked; each item names the existing mechanic it builds on.

1. **A hospitality bottleneck scene (content only, no engine work).** One
   guest-arrival sequence — knock, *mirë se erdhe → mirë se ju gjeta*, coffee
   offered with *urdhëro*, the *ha edhe pak / jam ngopur* insistence loop as an
   actual dialogue choice, *të bëftë mirë*, *gëzuar*, *faleminderit për
   gostinë* on leaving. This teaches the entire Tier-2 script as a *sequence*,
   which is how it occurs in life. The coffee/raki scene (`kafeja1`,
   `gezuarFund`) and NPC system already carry half the frame.

2. **Ear-first training mode.** The player's real-world gap is listening; the
   app already has per-word TTS clips and `speakSequence()` line narration.
   Add a Train question type that *plays* a word or a whole discovered line
   with the text hidden and asks for the meaning (or which word was said).
   Later, a "fast mode" that plays lines at natural cadence — the app's
   equivalent of dinner-table speed.

3. **Chunks as first-class senses.** `DICT` already supports multi-word
   entries (`natën e mirë`, `të lutem`). Add the Tier-1/Tier-2 chunks as
   entries rather than relying on word composition — *si je?* is a greeting,
   not a syntax exercise. The ti/ju pairs can share a gloss with a register
   tag, and the training distractors can exploit the contrast (answer *si
   jeni?* to the old man, *si je?* to the shepherd boy).

4. **"Say it tonight" card.** A small daily surface (one phrase from the
   tiers, with audio) framed as a quest: *use this at the table today*. Mark
   it done by self-report; repeat it sooner if not used. This is the app
   explicitly bridging to the learner's real classroom — scripted
   one-phrase-a-day routines are the single most-recommended practice in the
   partner-immersion literature.

5. **Register as a game mechanic.** NPCs already have ages and roles (the old
   man, the shepherd boy, the woman at the spring). Greeting an elder NPC with
   *ti* forms could cost standing/warmth where greeting with *ju/tungjatjeta*
   opens doors — making the generational register a learnable consequence
   instead of a footnote.

6. **Gheg "ear" variants (receptive only).** For a handful of core words,
   attach a second audio surface (*qysh*, *shpi*, *osht*) that appears only in
   listening drills, tagged as "how you might *hear* it". Production stays
   standard. `wf()` inflected-surface machinery is close to this already.

7. **A family lexicon hook.** Let the player add words they caught at the
   table (a mini "asked *si thuhet* today" inbox) — even a simple free-text
   list that feeds the existing training queue would close the loop between
   the household and the app.

Items 1–4 are the core of it: script, ear, chunks, bridge. Everything else is
polish on the same idea — the app stops being only a solo game and becomes the
learner's *preparation* for tonight's table.
